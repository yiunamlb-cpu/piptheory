# The Petrodollar, Explained (and the Myth of Its 2024 'Expiry')

The **petrodollar** is the name for the system under which global oil is priced, sold, and settled in US dollars — and under which the enormous revenues earned by oil exporters are reinvested into US financial assets. This informal arrangement, rooted in 1970s US-Saudi diplomacy, is one of the principal pillars propping up dollar demand worldwide. It is also one of the most misinformation-laden topics in macro finance: in 2024 a viral claim that a "50-year deal expired" spread across social media, was debunked by fact-checkers, and continues to circulate. This post explains what the petrodollar system actually is, how petrodollar recycling works, and precisely what the 2024 claim got wrong.

## What Is the Petrodollar System?

The petrodollar system is the set of arrangements — formal, informal, and market-driven — that ensure the global oil trade is denominated and settled in US dollars. Because nearly every country on earth imports oil, and oil is priced in dollars, every country must maintain dollar reserves simply to keep its energy supply running. That structural necessity generates constant, sustained global demand for USD that goes far beyond what trade in American goods and services alone would produce.

The system rests on three interlocking legs. First, oil exporters sell their crude in dollars, earning vast dollar surpluses. Second, those exporters recycle the surplus dollars back into US assets — principally <a href="https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/international/exchange-stabilization-fund" target="_blank" rel="noopener">US Treasury securities</a>, but also stocks, real estate, and private equity. Third, the US provides security guarantees and military cooperation to key producers, particularly Saudi Arabia, in return. The result is a self-reinforcing loop: dollar demand from oil buyers supports USD value; petrodollar recycling suppresses US borrowing costs; US security commitments keep the arrangement politically viable.

## How Did the Petrodollar Originate?

The petrodollar system grew out of the breakdown of the <a href="/research/nixon-shock-bretton-woods">Bretton Woods gold standard</a> and the 1973 Arab oil embargo. When Nixon closed the gold window in August 1971, the dollar lost its automatic anchor. Inflation spiked, the dollar slid, and the United States needed a new mechanism to underwrite global dollar demand.

In June 1974, the United States and Saudi Arabia signed a broad agreement on military and economic cooperation. According to reporting by <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2016-05-30/the-untold-story-behind-saudi-arabia-s-41-year-u-s-debt-secret" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bloomberg in May 2016</a>, the deal included a secret arrangement: Saudi Arabia would price its oil in dollars and recycle earnings into US Treasuries, and in return the United States would provide military equipment and security guarantees. It is important to note that oil had already been priced in dollars before 1974 — the arrangement formalised and deepened an existing practice rather than inventing it from scratch.

By the mid-1970s OPEC as a whole had aligned to dollar pricing. The system solidified. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, and Iraq alone held over <a href="https://www.currencytransfer.com/blog/expert-analysis/how-is-saudi-arabia-sustaining-dollar-dominance" target="_blank" rel="noopener">$271 billion in US Treasury securities</a> as of 2022 — a direct product of petrodollar recycling. Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund manages roughly $925 billion in assets, making it one of the largest sovereign wealth funds on earth, almost entirely seeded by petrodollar revenues.

## Why the Petrodollar Matters for FX and Currency Strength

Oil is the world's most traded commodity by value. A country running a current-account deficit that imports oil must sell its own currency and buy dollars — contributing to baseline USD demand that operates regardless of the interest-rate cycle or growth outlook. This is why the dollar tends to benefit in periods of high oil prices: more dollars are needed globally to pay for more expensive oil, reinforcing the currency's demand even as inflation bites.

The petrodollar system also constrains the Federal Reserve's policy freedom less than critics claim, but it does provide a structural bid for US Treasuries that suppresses long-term interest rates. Lower Treasury yields filter through to US mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, and consumer credit — a diffuse but real subsidy to the American economy.

## Debunking the 2024 "Petrodollar Expiry" Myth

In June 2024, a story spread rapidly across social media claiming that Saudi Arabia had let a "50-year petrodollar deal" expire on 9 June 2024, and that this signalled the imminent end of dollar dominance. The claim was amplified by financial bloggers, gold bugs, and geopolitical commentators. **It was false.**

<div class="pt-callout warn">
<span class="pt-callout-h">Viral claim — debunked</span>
The assertion that a secret 50-year formal US-Saudi petrodollar agreement expired on 9 June 2024 has been fact-checked and found to be false. No such binding 50-year treaty with an expiry clause has been identified in any US government record.
</div>

<a href="https://www.rfa.org/english/news/afcl/afcl-us-saudi-oil-deal-07082024043619.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Radio Free Asia's fact-check</a> found that no known formal deal stipulating that Saudi Arabia must sell oil in US dollars exists. An official at the US Government Accountability Office — the country's supreme auditing body — confirmed that the joint commission agreement that did expire in 2024 contained no formal language requiring oil trade in dollars. The <a href="https://intacapitalswiss.com/the-petrodollar-agreement-fact-or-fiction/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Inta Capital analysis</a> reached the same conclusion: the arrangement from 1974 was informal and kept deliberately secret, not a binding legal treaty with an expiry date.

What actually expired in 2024 was a US-Saudi joint economic commission established in the mid-1970s — a body that coordinated investment and economic cooperation. Its expiry was routine administrative housekeeping, not the termination of a secret dollar agreement. The claim conflated two entirely different things.

The Bloomberg 2016 investigation that first revealed the "secret" 1974 arrangements described them as informal understandings — the kind of implicit commitment that has held for fifty years not because of a legal clause but because both parties found it mutually advantageous.

## The Reality of Oil Invoicing Today

The petrodollar system is real, substantial, and still intact — but it is not monolithic, and it is slowly eroding at the margins. Here is a more accurate picture:

- **Dollar dominance persists.** The <a href="https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2024/06/11/dollar-dominance-in-the-international-reserve-system-an-update" target="_blank" rel="noopener">IMF confirmed</a> the dollar's share of global foreign-exchange reserves was about 58% in 2024, down from roughly 65% in 2001 but still far ahead of any rival.
- **Some non-dollar oil trades exist.** China now settles a portion of its crude imports — particularly from Russia and Iran — in yuan. Russia, under Western sanctions since 2022, has been forced to invoice more exports in non-dollar currencies. But these are geopolitically-driven exceptions, not a structural shift.
- **Saudi Arabia has diversified — but not abandoned dollars.** The kingdom has floated the idea of accepting other currencies, and its sovereign wealth fund invests globally. It has not, however, made any formal commitment to price Aramco crude in a non-dollar currency.
- **The infrastructure favours dollars.** Oil futures on the CME and ICE are dollar-denominated. Clearing, settlement, and hedging systems globally are built around the dollar. Replacing this infrastructure would take decades.

## What Petrodollar Recycling Means for Macro Traders

Petrodollar recycling creates a structural bid for US Treasuries that has suppressed the "term premium" — the extra yield investors demand for holding long-dated bonds. Lower term premiums mean lower long-term interest rates across the US economy. When oil revenues surge (as they did in 2022 when Brent briefly topped $120), petrodollar recycling accelerates and Gulf sovereign wealth funds buy more US assets, which is one reason the dollar strengthened even as US inflation hit 40-year highs. When oil revenues fall, that structural bid weakens.

For currency traders, the petrodollar dynamic matters because it creates a reflexive relationship between oil prices, the dollar, and commodity-linked currencies. Rising oil — more petrodollar demand for USD — tends to support the dollar even as it lifts commodity currencies like the <a href="/currency/cad">Canadian dollar</a> and the Norwegian krone. The direction of that tug-of-war depends on whether the dollar's structural bid from recycling outweighs the commodity-currency boost. Track how these dynamics play out in real time by watching the <a href="/pair/usdcad">USD/CAD pair</a> during oil-price moves.

One additional nuance: not all petrodollar recycling is neutral. Gulf SWFs are increasingly diversified investors — allocating to equity, private equity, and infrastructure globally, not just US Treasuries. Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 diversification agenda means a rising share of oil revenues is being deployed domestically rather than recycled into US assets. This secular shift, though slow, is a genuine, if modest, headwind to the structural Treasury bid that petrodollar recycling historically provided.
