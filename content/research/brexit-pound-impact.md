# Brexit and the Pound: The Biggest Sterling Shock in History

The **Brexit pound impact** was one of the most dramatic single-session currency moves in modern financial history. On the night of 23–24 June 2016, as it became clear that the United Kingdom had voted to leave the European Union, GBP/USD collapsed about **8% in under twelve hours** — from a referendum-day high of around $1.50 to roughly $1.32. That move was bigger than sterling's crisis exit from the ERM in 1992, bigger than its worst day during the 2008 financial crisis, and the largest one-session loss for any G10 currency since free-floating exchange rates began in the early 1970s.

## Why the pound was so exposed before the vote

Before the referendum, the British pound was already carrying structural vulnerabilities that made it sensitive to a surprise outcome. The UK was running a current-account deficit of about 5–7% of GDP — one of the largest among advanced economies — financed by inflows of foreign capital and portfolio investment. Much of that capital was attracted to UK assets partly because of the country's place inside the European single market: frictionless access to 450 million consumers, financial passporting for the City of London, and decades of EU-related foreign direct investment.

A vote to leave would put all of that at risk, requiring a fundamental re-pricing of UK growth prospects, the Bank of England's policy path, and sterling's equilibrium value. The City of London alone accounted for about 40% of all EU financial-services business transacted outside the bloc — a business that could theoretically be relocated to Frankfurt, Paris or Dublin if passporting rights were lost. The question for markets was not whether a Leave vote would be bad for sterling; it was whether a Leave vote would happen at all. Markets had decided it probably would not.

The pound had actually been softening since mid-2015 when the referendum was first called, falling from above $1.57 to about $1.42 by early 2016 as the probability of Leave was slowly incorporated. But through May and June 2016 it rallied back sharply toward $1.50 as Remain poll leads widened and bookmaker odds pushed the probability of Remain above 75–80%. That late rally was itself a vulnerability: it set up a much larger fall on a Leave result.

<div class="pt-callout info">
<span class="pt-callout-h">What the market was pricing</span>
On polling day itself, GBP/USD hit a <strong>2016 high of $1.5018</strong> — sterling was rallying on Remain optimism. Bookmaker odds implied a roughly 75–80% probability of Remain. A YouGov exit poll released at 10 pm on referendum night still showed Remain on 52%. The market was, in short, deeply positioned for the wrong outcome.
</div>

## The night the pound broke

Polls closed at 10 pm on 23 June 2016. For the first hour or two, sterling held firm. Then, as Leave results came in from Sunderland — a bellwether constituency — the picture changed fast.

By 8 am on 24 June, the pound had fallen to about $1.36 — down from nearly $1.50 the previous night, a historic 8% collapse in under twelve hours. [According to the LSE's Centre for Economic Performance](https://cep.lse.ac.uk/pubs/download/dp1890.pdf), this was the biggest two-hour move sterling had ever suffered and the largest single-day loss for any G10 currency since the free-float era began.

## Why the move was so large

The sheer size of the overnight collapse can be understood through three lenses:

**1. Complete mispricing of risk.** When a market prices an 80% probability of Remain and Leave wins, the move is not just the "fair value" repricing — it is the unwinding of all the accumulated Remain-biased positions plus the fresh repricing. The more one-sided the positioning, the bigger the snap-back.

**2. Structural complexity of the repricing.** Leaving the EU meant re-pricing UK trade flows, UK financial services passporting, potential Scottish independence, Bank of England policy (cut or hike?), and the long-term equilibrium current-account funding model. Markets cannot solve all of that in real time; they sell first and ask questions later.

**3. Illiquidity at 2 am.** The bulk of the move happened in the early hours of the morning when Asian liquidity desks are thin. Large flows in a thin market produce violent moves. This is a recurring pattern in political-shock FX events.

## The longer-term sterling story

Sterling's trade-weighted index — a broader measure of its value against all major trading partners, tracked by the Bank of England — fell about 10–15% in the weeks after the vote and remained at multi-decade lows for several years. The pound's trade-weighted index only recovered to levels last seen during the 2008–2013 period, well below its pre-2016 level.

The weaker pound had a direct economic consequence: higher import prices fed through into UK consumer price inflation, contributing to a squeeze on real household incomes in 2017 and 2018. The [Bank of England](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk) initially cut rates in August 2016 (to 0.25%, the lowest in its 322-year history at the time) to cushion the shock, then eventually had to start raising them as inflation picked up.

<div class="pt-callout key">
<span class="pt-callout-h">The macro lesson</span>
Political shocks can move currencies more violently than almost any economic data release — because they reprice multiple fundamentals at once. Brexit repriced UK trade, UK growth, UK rates and UK financial-sector revenues simultaneously, overnight. No economic indicator could have given you that in advance; only recognising the binary nature of the risk could.
</div>

## What this means for macro FX traders

The Brexit episode contains several enduring lessons for anyone thinking about currency risk:

<ol class="pt-steps">
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Binary political events need explicit hedging</span> Unlike economic data surprises, referendum and election results are binary. The options market was expensive before the vote but correctly priced the magnitude of the risk — the implied volatility was rational in hindsight.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Positioning creates the amplification</span> The more uniformly a market is positioned, the larger the snap-back on a surprise. GBP was long across the board ahead of the vote, amplifying the fall.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Fundamental repricing takes months, not hours</span> The immediate overnight move priced the shock; the multi-year trade-weighted decline reflected the slow repricing of UK trade, growth and investment flows as the reality of Brexit negotiations became clear.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Central banks can only cushion, not reverse</span> The Bank of England could ease policy to reduce the GDP hit — and did — but it could not restore sterling to its pre-vote level. Macro policy can smooth a curve; it cannot undo a structural repricing.</li>
</ol>

## The euro, the SNB and a broader context

Brexit did not happen in isolation. The pound's vulnerability was partly a product of its place in the broader G10 currency landscape, where political uncertainty functions as a direct discount rate on a currency. The Swiss franc, for example, surged on Brexit night because of its safe-haven status — a pattern explored in detail in the [Swiss Franc Shock of 2015](/research/swiss-franc-shock-2015) article, where the SNB's decision to remove the EUR/CHF floor produced a similarly violent single-day move.

What makes the Brexit case distinctive is that it was entirely a **political risk event** — not a macroeconomic deterioration or a central-bank policy mistake. The fundamentals of the UK economy in June 2016 were not dramatically different from the month before. What changed was the structure of the UK's relationship with its largest trading partner, repriced instantly. That distinction matters for how macro traders think about political event risk versus fundamental currency weakness: the tools are different, and the timescales are different.

A currency that is fundamentally weak will tend to drift lower over months or years as the macro backdrop erodes. A currency hit by a political shock can fall 8% in a night, then partially recover, then grind lower again over years as the structural consequences play out. Both patterns are real; both require a different analytical lens. Understanding which type of risk is driving a currency's move is half the analytical work.
