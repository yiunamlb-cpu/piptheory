# What Moves the British Pound (GBP)? The Forces Behind Sterling

The **British pound** (GBP, or sterling) is the world's fourth most-traded currency and carries a history stretching back further than any other major in active circulation. Yet for all its heritage, the pound is one of the most **politically volatile** and **risk-sensitive** currencies among the G7 majors. Understanding **what drives the British pound** means looking at Bank of England policy, the UK's persistent reliance on foreign capital, and the episodes of political turbulence that have given sterling its reputation for sudden, violent moves.

<li>The Bank of England's interest rate decisions and UK gilt yields are sterling's primary macro drivers.</li>
<li>The UK's chronic <strong>current-account deficit</strong> (~2% of GDP) makes sterling structurally dependent on foreign investor confidence.</li>
<li>GBP is a <strong>high-beta, risk-sensitive</strong> currency — it falls sharply in global risk-off events and domestic political crises.</li>
<li>The <strong>September 2022 mini-budget</strong> sent sterling to an all-time low of $1.0327, triggering a simultaneous gilt crisis that required BoE emergency intervention.</li>
<li>Unlike the CHF, JPY, or USD, sterling is <strong>not a safe-haven currency</strong>.</li>
</ul>

## Bank of England policy and gilt yields

The <a href="https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bank of England</a> sets the UK base rate, which anchors the yield on UK government bonds (gilts) and determines the relative attractiveness of pound-denominated assets to global investors. When BoE rates are high — or rising — relative to peers, capital flows into gilts and sterling tends to strengthen. When rates are cut, or when the market expects the BoE to cut before the Fed or ECB, sterling typically weakens.

The post-pandemic tightening cycle illustrated both the power and the limits of this mechanism. The BoE began raising rates in December 2021 — ahead of both the Fed and ECB — and ultimately took the base rate from 0.1% to a <a href="https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy/the-interest-rate-bank-rate" target="_blank" rel="noopener">15-year high of 5.25% by August 2023</a>. Sterling was broadly supported during the hike cycle. But the pound's performance was repeatedly interrupted by political events and supply-side shocks that undermined confidence in UK fiscal management — most dramatically in autumn 2022.

For more on how central bank rate decisions transmit into currency moves, see [bond yields and currencies](/research/bond-yields-and-currencies) and the broader [how central banks move currencies](/research/how-central-banks-move-currencies) explainer.

## The current-account deficit: sterling's structural vulnerability

One of the most important — and most frequently underappreciated — drivers of sterling is the **UK's chronic current-account deficit**. The current account tracks the flow of goods, services, and income across UK borders. The UK consistently imports more than it exports and receives less in investment income than it pays out. According to the <a href="https://www.ons.gov.uk/economy/nationalaccounts/balanceofpayments/bulletins/unitedkingdombalanceofpaymentsthepinkbook/2024" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Office for National Statistics</a>, the UK ran a current-account deficit of roughly **2.0% of GDP in 2023** — the second largest in the G7, after the United States.

This structural deficit means the UK must continuously attract foreign capital — through foreign direct investment, portfolio investment in gilts and UK equities, and borrowing — just to keep the exchange rate stable. Sterling is, in the phrase often used by analysts, dependent on the **"kindness of strangers."** When global investors are willing to hold and accumulate UK assets, the inflows more than fund the deficit. When confidence falters — because of political instability, a credibility shock to UK fiscal policy, or a broader global risk-off — those inflows dry up and sterling can fall sharply and quickly.

For the full analytical framework on current accounts and currencies, see [current account and currencies](/research/current-account-and-currencies).

<div class="pt-callout warn">
<span class="pt-callout-h">Why the UK deficit matters more than most</span>
A country with a current-account surplus (like Japan) can withstand capital outflows by repatriating its own overseas wealth. The UK cannot — it has no large pool of foreign assets to draw on. Every bout of investor uncertainty is therefore magnified in sterling because the funding mechanism for the deficit is discretionary, not structural.
</div>

## GBP as a high-beta, risk-sensitive currency

Sterling is widely classified as a **high-beta currency** in FX markets — meaning it tends to amplify the direction of global risk sentiment. When equity markets are rallying, the global economy is growing strongly, and investors are comfortable taking risk, GBP often outperforms; when global risk appetite retreats, sterling tends to underperform. This is the opposite of safe-haven behaviour: where the Swiss franc or Japanese yen typically strengthen when markets panic, the pound often falls.

The reason is structural: the UK's open capital account, large financial sector, and reliance on portfolio inflows mean that any broad rotation away from risk assets tends to include UK assets. GBP/JPY — sometimes called "the beast" by traders — captures this cleanly: it combines a high-beta currency (GBP) with a safe-haven/funding currency (JPY) and often moves with extreme volatility during risk events.

## Political risk episodes: from Black Wednesday to Brexit to the mini-budget

The pound has a long history of sudden, policy-driven crises. Three stand out as the most instructive.

### Black Wednesday, 16 September 1992

On 16 September 1992, the British government was forced to withdraw sterling from the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) after failing to defend the currency's floor against a speculative attack led, most famously, by George Soros. The pound fell approximately 15% against the Deutsche Mark in the days around the crisis. For the full story of how Soros broke the Bank of England, see [Black Wednesday 1992](/research/black-wednesday-1992-soros).

### Brexit, 23 June 2016

The UK's vote to leave the European Union on 23 June 2016 triggered one of sterling's sharpest single-day falls in history — GBP/USD dropped from around $1.50 to below $1.33 in the immediate aftermath, a fall of more than 10% within hours. The pound remained structurally depressed for years as Brexit uncertainty weighed on UK growth and investment. The full analysis is in [Brexit and the pound](/research/brexit-pound-impact).

### The September 2022 mini-budget

The most instructive modern episode for understanding sterling's sensitivity to fiscal credibility is the September 2022 mini-budget under Prime Minister Liz Truss and Chancellor Kwasi Kwarteng. On <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/September_2022_United_Kingdom_mini-budget" target="_blank" rel="noopener">23 September 2022</a>, the government announced approximately **£45 billion in unfunded tax cuts** — the largest package in 50 years — alongside large-scale borrowing commitments. The market reaction was immediate and violent.

The episode demonstrated how quickly sterling can price a fiscal credibility shock: the <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-23/liz-truss-s-historic-gamble-with-the-uk-economy-is-already-unraveling" target="_blank" rel="noopener">IMF took the unusual step</a> of publicly criticising the budget, bond markets moved faster than policymakers could respond, and the gilt crisis spread from the currency market into the pension system. For a macro trader, the episode is a textbook case of what happens when a country with a large current-account deficit announces an unfinanced fiscal expansion without independent central bank cover.

## What to watch to gauge sterling's direction

Reading the pound requires monitoring multiple dimensions simultaneously. Three are most critical.

<ol class="pt-steps">
<li><span class="pt-step-h">BoE rate path vs peers</span> Is the BoE hiking, holding, or cutting — and is it ahead of or behind the Fed and ECB? The rate differential versus the US (GBP/USD) and the eurozone (GBP/EUR, or EUR/GBP inverted) sets the capital flow incentive.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">UK fiscal credibility</span> Budget announcements, Office for Budget Responsibility forecasts, and debt sustainability assessments all affect gilt yields and, by extension, sterling. A credible fiscal path supports inflows; a profligate one risks a confidence crisis of the kind seen in September 2022.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Global risk sentiment</span> Because GBP is high-beta, broad global risk-off episodes (equity selloffs, credit stress, geopolitical shocks) tend to hurt sterling even when UK fundamentals are unchanged. Watch GBP alongside AUD and NZD — they often move in the same direction in risk events.</li>
</ol>
