# What Drives EUR/USD? Inside the World's Most-Traded Currency Pair

EUR/USD is the most liquid, most-traded currency pair on earth. According to the <a href="https://www.bis.org/statistics/rpfx22.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BIS 2022 Triennial Central Bank Survey</a>, it accounted for **22.7% of global daily FX turnover** out of a market averaging $7.5 trillion per day — meaning roughly one in every four dollars traded in FX involves this pair. Understanding **what moves EUR/USD** is, in practice, understanding much of the modern macro FX landscape.

At its core, EUR/USD is a contest between two of the world's most powerful central banks — the <a href="https://www.ecb.europa.eu" target="_blank" rel="noopener">European Central Bank</a> and the <a href="https://www.federalreserve.gov" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Federal Reserve</a>. The pair rises when the eurozone's macro backdrop improves relative to the US, and falls when the reverse is true. Four forces do most of the work: interest rate differentials, relative growth, energy and terms of trade, and global risk sentiment.

## How Big Is EUR/USD? A Market in a Class of Its Own

No other currency pair comes close to EUR/USD for depth and liquidity. At $7.5 trillion in daily global FX trading in April 2022, the EUR/USD slice alone — roughly $1.7 trillion per day — dwarfs many entire national stock markets. That liquidity matters in practical terms: bid-ask spreads are the tightest in the FX world, large institutional orders move through without slippage, and the pair reacts immediately to every macro data release from Washington or Frankfurt.

The pair's dominance reflects the size of the two underlying economies. The eurozone and United States together account for a substantial share of global GDP, trade, and capital markets. Any institution managing international assets, hedging European trade flows, or taking macro positions in developed-market rates will almost inevitably touch EUR/USD. ## What Moves EUR/USD? The Fed-ECB Rate Differential

The single most important driver of EUR/USD in the short to medium term is the **interest rate differential** between the Federal Reserve and the ECB. When the Fed funds rate is materially higher than the ECB's deposit rate, dollar-denominated assets offer better carry, drawing global capital toward the US and pushing EUR/USD lower. When the ECB tightens more aggressively — or the Fed pivots toward easing — the dynamic reverses.

The 2022 experience is the clearest modern case study. The Fed began hiking in March 2022 and moved rapidly. The ECB defended loose policy much longer, finally raising rates for the first time in 11 years on <a href="https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/date/2022/html/ecb.mp220721~53e5bdd317.en.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">21 July 2022</a>, with that first move ending the era of negative rates. By the time the ECB started catching up, the rate gap had already driven significant euro weakness.

## Relative Growth: The Medium-Term Rudder

Beyond rates, **relative economic growth** is the medium-term anchor. When US GDP, employment, and business surveys (ISM/PMI) outpace their eurozone equivalents (PMIs, German Ifo, eurozone GDP), the dollar tends to strengthen on expectations of tighter future Fed policy and stronger capital inflows.

The reverse also holds. The 2017 euro rally — EUR/USD climbed from around $1.04 in early 2017 to above $1.20 by year-end — was partly explained by a sharp improvement in eurozone PMIs as the post-debt-crisis recovery broadened. The growth gap narrowed substantially, and the market began pricing ECB normalisation before it arrived, lifting the euro well ahead of any actual policy change.

## The Energy Channel: Europe's Structural Vulnerability

The eurozone is a large **net energy importer**. Europe buys oil priced in dollars and gas from producers whose revenues are dollar-linked, meaning a surge in energy prices simultaneously (1) worsens Europe's trade balance, (2) raises inflation in a way that hurts real growth, and (3) increases demand for dollars to pay energy bills. All three effects weaken the euro.

This energy vulnerability became decisive in 2022. Russia's invasion of Ukraine triggered a gas supply shock after <a href="https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/economic-bulletin/focus/2022/html/ecb.ebbox202204_01~68ef3c3dc6.en.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Russia cut approximately 80 billion cubic metres of pipeline gas to Europe</a>, sending energy prices to records. EUR/USD, which started 2022 near $1.14, fell through parity for the first time since 2002 in July, and reached a 20-year low of approximately $0.9535 in September 2022 as the Nord Stream 1 pipeline was indefinitely shut.

<span class="pt-callout-h">The 2022 energy shock in numbers</span>
EUR/USD fell roughly 16% from its 2022 open of ~$1.137 to its September low near $0.9535. ## Risk Sentiment and the Dollar Smile

EUR/USD does not just move on eurozone-versus-US fundamentals. It also moves on **global risk sentiment** — and in a specific, asymmetric way described by former Morgan Stanley strategist <a href="https://www.eurizonsljcapital.com/dollar-smile/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Stephen Jen's "dollar smile" theory</a>.

The smile works like this: the dollar strengthens at both ends of the risk spectrum. On the left, in global crises or sharp risk-off episodes, capital flees to the dollar as the world's reserve safe haven — EUR/USD falls even if Europe is not the epicentre. On the right, when the US economy is growing faster than the rest of the world, it attracts capital for fundamental reasons and EUR/USD also falls. Only in the middle — when global growth is synchronised and stable — does the dollar tend to ease and EUR/USD gain sustained ground.

This pattern matters for reading EUR/USD moves. A sudden EUR/USD drop does not always mean the eurozone deteriorated — sometimes it simply means global risk appetite collapsed and the dollar attracted safe-haven flows. Distinguishing a fundamental shift from a sentiment spike is exactly what a macro [currency strength meter](/) is designed to help with.

## The Long Run: Relative Inflation and Fair Value

Over horizons of years to decades, EUR/USD gravitates toward a range implied by **relative purchasing power parity**: if US inflation persistently runs higher than eurozone inflation, the dollar should, over time, depreciate to compensate (and vice versa). This is the slow gravitational force that prevents the pair from drifting arbitrarily far from economic reality, even as short-term drivers dominate.

The <a href="https://www.oecd.org/en/data/datasets/purchasing-power-parities.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OECD's PPP exchange rate data</a> provides an institutional benchmark for where EUR/USD "should" sit based on relative price levels. As with all PPP measures, deviations from fair value can persist for years — this is not a short-term trading tool. For a thorough treatment of PPP as an FX framework, see the [purchasing power parity explainer](/research/purchasing-power-parity-explained).

| Driver | Dominant timescale | Direction when US stronger than eurozone |
|---|---|---|
| Fed-ECB rate differential | Weeks – months | EUR/USD falls |
| Relative growth outlook | Months – 1–2 years | EUR/USD falls |
| Energy / terms-of-trade | Months (can persist) | EUR/USD falls (if energy rises) |
| Risk sentiment / dollar smile | Days – weeks | EUR/USD falls in crises |
| Relative inflation (PPP) | Years – decades | USD depreciates if US inflation higher |

## What the 2022 Move Teaches

The 2022 episode is worth studying because all four negative-euro forces fired simultaneously: the Fed hiked faster (rate differential), the US economy was more insulated from the gas shock (growth differential), energy prices hit Europe's trade balance (terms of trade), and global risk appetite was compressed by tightening financial conditions (risk sentiment). The result was the fastest and deepest EUR/USD decline in a generation.

## Putting It Together for Traders

EUR/USD is driven by the interaction of all four forces, rarely by one alone. The practical checklist for framing a EUR/USD view:

<ol class="pt-steps">
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Rate differential</span> Where is the Fed funds rate versus the ECB deposit rate, and which direction is each central bank heading? Forward curves embedded in 2-year bond spreads (US 2-year minus German 2-year) are the cleanest real-time proxy.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Growth gap</span> Are US PMIs and GDP running ahead of or behind eurozone equivalents? A widening US advantage is typically euro-negative.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Energy / trade balance</span> Is Europe facing an energy supply shock? A terms-of-trade deterioration weakens the euro's structural support.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">Risk environment</span> Is the dollar smile's left side (crisis) or right side (US outperformance) in play? If so, dollar strength may be a sentiment phenomenon, not a lasting fundamental shift.</li>
<li><span class="pt-step-h">PPP anchor</span> How far has the pair moved from PPP-implied fair value? Large deviations can persist, but they eventually correct.</li>
</ol>

<div class="pt-callout key">
<span class="pt-callout-h">The key insight</span>
EUR/USD is a macro thermometer as much as an exchange rate. When all four drivers align — rate differential, growth gap, energy, and risk sentiment — the moves are large and sustained, as 2022 demonstrated. When they pull in different directions, the pair tends to chop. Reading the alignment of drivers, not just the latest data print, is what separates a reasoned macro view from noise.
