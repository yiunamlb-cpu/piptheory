# The Nixon Shock and the End of Bretton Woods (1971)

The **Nixon shock** is the name historians and economists give to the measures President Richard Nixon announced on the evening of 15 August 1971 — most importantly, the suspension of the US dollar's convertibility to gold. With that single decision, Nixon dismantled the post-World War II monetary order established at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire in 1944, and set the world on a path to the freely floating exchange rates that govern FX markets to this day. It was one of the most consequential acts in the history of money — and understanding it is essential context for anyone trying to make sense of why currencies move as they do.

<li>By March 1973, floating exchange rates were the new global standard. FX markets as we know them were born.</li>

## What Was the Bretton Woods System?

The Bretton Woods system was designed at a conference attended by representatives from 44 Allied nations in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, in July 1944, as World War II was still being fought. The delegates — led by the American economist Harry Dexter White and the British economist John Maynard Keynes — knew they needed a monetary order that would prevent the competitive devaluations and protectionism of the 1930s from ever recurring.

Their solution was elegantly simple: <a href="https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/gold-convertibility-ends" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the US dollar was fixed to gold at $35 per ounce</a>, and every other major currency was fixed to the dollar at agreed exchange rates. Foreign governments and central banks could present dollars to the US Treasury and receive gold at that fixed price. The dollar was, in effect, a warehouse receipt for gold — and all other currencies were warehouse receipts for dollars.

The system made the United States the anchor of the global economy. It also made the dollar the world's primary reserve currency: countries held dollars rather than gold because dollars were as good as gold (legally convertible) and far more useful for trade. The International Monetary Fund was created at the same conference to oversee the system and provide balance-of-payments support to countries in difficulty.

<div class="pt-callout info">
<span class="pt-callout-h">Why Bretton Woods worked — at first</span>
After World War II the US held about two-thirds of the world's gold reserves and ran large trade surpluses. Dollar scarcity, not dollar excess, was the initial problem. The US Marshall Plan deliberately pumped dollars into war-ravaged Europe to lubricate recovery. The system worked because American economic dominance was unchallenged.
</div>

## The Triffin Dilemma: The Flaw Built Into the System

By 1960, Belgian-American economist Robert Triffin had identified a structural paradox that would eventually destroy Bretton Woods. In his congressional testimony and his book *Gold and the Dollar Crisis*, Triffin argued that the dollar's role as the world's reserve currency was inherently self-contradicting.

To supply the world with the liquidity it needed for growing trade, the US had to run balance-of-payments deficits — spending and lending abroad more than it earned. But those deficits were accumulating dollars in foreign hands. As foreign dollar holdings grew, they would eventually exceed US gold reserves, making the promise to convert dollars to gold at $35 an ounce impossible to keep. The more the world used dollars, the less the gold backing was worth.

Triffin's analysis was prescient. Through the 1960s, US spending on the Vietnam War, the Great Society social programmes, and the space race produced growing fiscal deficits. The balance of payments deteriorated. France, under President de Gaulle, adopted an aggressive policy of converting dollar reserves into gold, challenging what de Gaulle called the dollar's "exorbitant privilege." Other countries followed. By August 1971, the US held only about <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_shock" target="_blank" rel="noopener">10,000 metric tonnes of gold</a> — less than half of its peak — against far larger foreign dollar holdings.

## 15 August 1971: Nixon Closes the Gold Window

The immediate trigger was a run on US gold. In the summer of 1971, the United States was running a trade deficit for the first time since the 19th century, inflation was rising, and speculators were betting that the dollar would have to be devalued. Foreign central banks were presenting dollars for gold at an accelerating pace.

From 13 to 15 August 1971, Nixon convened a secret meeting of fifteen advisers at Camp David — including Federal Reserve Chairman Arthur Burns, Treasury Secretary John Connally, and Undersecretary Paul Volcker (later Fed Chairman) — to devise a response. What emerged was a package of measures announced to the American people on the evening of 15 August in a televised address.

The centrepiece was the suspension of gold convertibility: foreign governments would no longer be able to exchange their dollars for gold at $35 an ounce. Additional measures included a 90-day freeze on wages and prices, a 10% surcharge on imports, and spending cuts. Nixon framed the announcement as a decisive domestic economic intervention; the international monetary implications were treated almost as an afterthought.

## The Smithsonian Agreement: A Temporary Patch

In December 1971, the Group of Ten industrial nations gathered at the Smithsonian Institution in Washington to attempt to restore some version of fixed rates. The <a href="https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/smithsonian-agreement" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Smithsonian Agreement</a> involved a devaluation of the dollar to $38 per ounce of gold (a 7.9% devaluation from $35), wider trading bands of 2.25% either side of parity, and appreciations by trading partners — the Japanese yen by 16.9% and the Deutsche Mark by 13.6%. Nixon declared it "the greatest monetary agreement in the history of the world."

It lasted barely fifteen months. The underlying problems had not been solved: US inflation was rising, the balance of payments was still in deficit, and there was no gold backing for the new rates. In February 1973 a further dollar devaluation to $42.22 per ounce was announced. It was not enough. Within weeks, Japan and then the European Community abandoned fixed rates and allowed their currencies to float. By March 1973, the Bretton Woods fixed-rate system was definitively over.

## What the Nixon Shock Means for FX Today

The Nixon shock gave the world the floating exchange-rate system that every currency trader operates within. After 1973, market forces — capital flows, interest-rate differentials, trade balances, risk sentiment — rather than government decrees, determined exchange rates. This created both enormous opportunity and enormous volatility.

Several enduring consequences shaped today's FX landscape:

**The dollar remained dominant without gold.** Rather than collapsing as critics feared, the dollar maintained its reserve status through sheer network effects, the depth of US capital markets, and — from the mid-1970s onward — the <a href="/research/petrodollar-explained">petrodollar system</a>, which tied oil trade to USD. The <a href="/research/reserve-currencies-explained">reserve-currency</a> role was sustained by utility, not a metal peg.

**Central banks gained policy freedom — and responsibility.** Without the gold anchor, monetary policy became a tool for managing inflation and growth directly. The subsequent decade of stagflation forced central banks (led by the <a href="https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/gold-convertibility-ends" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Volcker Fed</a>) to develop the credibility frameworks — inflation targeting, independent central banking — that define modern monetary policy.

**Competitive pressures created new institutions.** The <a href="/research/plaza-accord-explained">Plaza Accord of 1985</a> and the Louvre Accord of 1987 were direct responses to the excessive dollar volatility that floating rates produced. The IMF's role shifted from policing fixed parities to providing crisis financing and macroeconomic surveillance.

**The FX market expanded massively.** Fixed rates required only occasional intervention; floating rates created a continuous need for hedging, speculation, and liquidity provision. The global foreign-exchange market grew from a narrow interbank network to the world's largest financial market by turnover, now exceeding $7.5 trillion per day according to the <a href="https://www.bis.org/statistics/rpfx22.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BIS Triennial Survey</a>.

<span class="pt-callout-h">The lasting lesson</span>
The Nixon shock was not a policy choice so much as an inevitable reckoning with the Triffin dilemma. No country can simultaneously maintain a credible gold peg, run the deficits needed to supply the world with reserve currency, and avoid a confidence crisis. Floating rates were not a design — they were the outcome of a system that could not be fixed.
