# What Drives the Euro (EUR)? The Forces Behind the World's #2 Currency

The **euro** is the world's second most-held reserve currency, the official currency of 20 EU member states, and the largest single component of the US Dollar Index — giving it outsized weight in global forex markets. Understanding **what drives the euro** means understanding ECB policy, the rate differential between Frankfurt and Washington, European energy dependence, and the fragile geometry of a single currency shared across very different economies.

<div class="pt-tldr">
<div class="pt-tldr-h">Key takeaways</div>
<ul>
<li>The ECB's interest rate decisions and the Fed–ECB rate differential are the euro's primary driver.</li>
<li>The euro is highly sensitive to <strong>energy prices</strong> — Europe imports most of its energy, and energy shocks hit the terms of trade and growth outlook hard.</li>
<li>Peripheral bond spreads (Italy–Germany, Spain–Germany) act as a stress gauge for eurozone integrity; wide spreads typically weaken the euro.</li>
<li>EUR/USD behaves as the <strong>"anti-dollar"</strong>: when the US dollar broadly strengthens (Fed tightening, risk-off), EUR/USD falls.</li>
<li>EUR/USD fell to a 20-year low near <strong>$0.9601 in September 2022</strong> during the energy crisis and peak Fed-ECB divergence.</li>
</ul>
</div>

## ECB policy: the primary driver

The <a href="https://www.ecb.europa.eu/home/html/index.en.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">European Central Bank's</a> interest rate stance is the largest single driver of the euro's value. When the ECB raises rates, euro-denominated assets offer a higher return, attracting global capital and supporting the currency. When it cuts or holds while other central banks tighten, the euro tends to weaken as capital migrates toward the higher-yielding alternative.

The 2022 episode illustrated this with unusual clarity. The ECB raised rates for the first time in 11 years in <a href="https://www.cnbc.com/2022/07/21/european-central-bank-raises-rates-by-50-basis-points-its-first-hike-in-11-years.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">July 2022</a> — and the first hike was 50 basis points, a larger step than the ECB had signalled, driven by inflation running near double-digit levels. But the ECB had defended its loose monetary policy well into 2022 while the Fed began hiking in March, creating a widening interest rate differential that pushed the euro below parity with the dollar for the first time since 2002.

## The energy shock: 2022's defining driver

Energy is one of the euro's most powerful and most underappreciated drivers. The eurozone is a large net energy importer — it relies heavily on external supplies of oil, gas, and coal. When energy prices surge, Europe pays more for the same imports, worsening the current account balance and slowing growth. Both effects are negative for the euro.

The 2022 energy crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine was the starkest modern example. European natural gas prices, which had traded around 25 euros per MWh in pre-crisis years, surged to an average of roughly <a href="https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/euro-weakness-2022" target="_blank" rel="noopener">130 euros per MWh in 2022</a>, according to CEPR analysis. The eurozone's trade surplus evaporated and the growth outlook deteriorated sharply. EUR/USD broke below parity in July 2022 — the first time in 20 years — and hit a year-to-date low of **$0.9601 on 27 September 2022**, following the indefinite shutdown of the Nord Stream 1 pipeline that month.

<span class="pt-callout-h">Energy as an FX driver</span>
Unlike commodity-exporting currencies (AUD, CAD, NOK) that benefit from rising commodity prices, the euro is a commodity-importing currency. Higher oil and gas prices are structurally negative for EUR/USD, particularly when the supply shock is geopolitical rather than demand-driven.

Once the ECB responded with aggressive rate hikes — including a 75 basis-point move in October 2022 — and energy markets began to stabilise, EUR/USD recovered above parity and reached $1.07 by year-end, then continued climbing into 2023 as the energy crisis eased.

## Peripheral spreads and fragmentation risk

A structural vulnerability unique to the euro is **fragmentation risk** — the danger that ECB tightening causes borrowing costs to diverge so sharply across eurozone member states that the monetary union itself comes under stress. When the ECB raises its benchmark rate, it does so for the whole eurozone. But Italian, Spanish, and Greek sovereign bond yields may rise much faster than German Bund yields if markets sense fiscal or political risk in those countries, creating a "fragmentation" of financial conditions across the bloc.

The **spread between Italian BTP and German Bund 10-year yields** is the standard barometer for fragmentation risk. When this spread widens sharply — as it did in 2010–12 (the eurozone debt crisis) and again briefly in 2022 — it signals that markets are pricing renewed existential risk to the euro, and the currency typically weakens in response.

The ECB's response to the 2022 resurgence of this risk was to announce the <a href="https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/date/2022/html/ecb.pr220721~973e6e7273.en.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Transmission Protection Instrument (TPI)</a> in July 2022 — a bond-buying facility that can purchase sovereign bonds of members facing "unwarranted, disorderly" spread widening, with no pre-announced cap on the amounts. The TPI's mere existence as a credible backstop helped stabilise peripheral spreads even before any purchases were made, demonstrating the power of forward guidance as a monetary tool.

## Growth differentials versus the US

Beyond energy and rates, the euro responds to the **relative economic health** of the eurozone versus the United States. When US growth consistently outpaces eurozone growth — whether measured by PMI surveys, GDP prints, or labour market data — global capital tends to prefer dollar-denominated assets, weakening EUR/USD. The reverse applies when European growth momentum strengthens or US growth disappoints.

This growth differential helps explain why EUR/USD is often described as the **"anti-dollar"** — it is not just that euro and dollar are inversely related mechanically, but that any macro tailwind for the US (faster growth, Fed hikes, safer haven status) tends to come at the euro's expense, and vice versa. Because the euro makes up 57.6% of the DXY, this inverse relationship is nearly structural.

## EUR/USD: the world's most-traded currency pair

EUR/USD accounts for roughly 23% of all forex trading volume globally, according to <a href="https://www.bis.org/statistics/rpfx22.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BIS Triennial Survey data</a>. It is the benchmark pair for global macro positioning, and moves in EUR/USD often set the tone for other dollar pairs. Macro funds and central banks alike treat it as the primary expression of the Fed–ECB policy divergence trade.

For anyone tracking macro currency strength, the EUR/USD is also one of the most reliable indicators of the broader dollar cycle. When the euro is strengthening against a basket of currencies and EUR/USD is climbing, it is usually a sign that either the ECB is hawkish relative to the Fed, European growth is surprising to the upside, or the dollar itself is losing some of its safe-haven premium. ## What to watch to gauge euro direction

Macro traders and analysts monitor several overlapping signals when building a view on EUR.

**ECB meeting decisions and forward guidance** are the highest-frequency catalyst. Markets price the expected path of ECB rates continuously, and any shift in the tone of ECB President Christine Lagarde's post-meeting press conference — more hawkish or more dovish than anticipated — can move EUR/USD by 50 to 100 basis points within hours. Reading the ECB's monthly economic projections and their revised growth and inflation forecasts provides early clues about the direction of future hikes or cuts.

**German Bund yields** are the eurozone's risk-free rate benchmark, analogous to US Treasuries. When Bund yields rise in tandem with ECB hikes and eurozone growth is firm, the euro tends to find support. When Bund yields fall (risk-off, ECB cutting, or slowing growth) while US Treasury yields hold, the EUR/USD rate differential compresses against the euro.

**PMI surveys** — particularly the Eurozone Composite PMI published by S&P Global — are among the most market-moving data releases for EUR/USD. A reading above 50 signals expansion; below 50 signals contraction. A sharp drop in the eurozone PMI relative to the US ISM typically accelerates EUR/USD selling, while a eurozone PMI surprise to the upside relative to the US counterpart can trigger significant euro strength.

**Energy price trends** remain a persistent background variable. Europe's dependence on imported energy means that a re-escalation of global energy prices — from geopolitical disruption, supply constraints, or a cold winter — is a structural headwind for the euro that does not affect the dollar or pound in the same way. Traders tracking EUR/USD over multi-week horizons should always have European natural gas prices (the TTF benchmark) on their radar alongside the standard FX data.

Finally, **the broader dollar cycle** matters as much as any EUR-specific driver. Because EUR/USD makes up 57.6% of the DXY, a broad dollar strengthening episode — even one driven purely by US factors like Fed hawkishness or safe-haven demand — will push EUR/USD lower regardless of eurozone conditions. This is why analysing EUR alongside the [US dollar's macro score](/currency/usd) and reading the [broader dollar cycle context](/research/us-dollar-index-dxy-explained) is essential to avoid misattributing dollar moves as euro-specific stories.

<span class="pt-callout-h">The three questions for the euro</span>
When assessing EUR direction, ask: (1) Is the ECB raising, holding, or cutting relative to the Fed? (2) What is the energy price environment — is Europe's import bill rising or falling? (3) Are peripheral bond spreads stable, narrowing, or widening? The answers to those three questions explain the majority of EUR/USD's major moves.
