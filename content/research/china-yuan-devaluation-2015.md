# China's 2015 Yuan Devaluation Shock, Explained

On the morning of **11 August 2015**, the People's Bank of China reset its daily yuan reference rate 1.9% weaker — the single largest one-day move since China unified its dual exchange rates in 1994. By the time the dust settled three days later, the yuan had depreciated roughly 3% in total, global equities had sold off, the Australian dollar had fallen sharply, and emerging-market currencies from Indonesia to Brazil were under pressure. What looked to Beijing like a controlled, technically necessary reform landed on global markets like a devaluation shock. Understanding why requires unpacking how China's exchange-rate system actually worked — and what the PBoC was trying to achieve.

## How did China's exchange rate system work before August 2015?

China did not have a floating exchange rate. The PBoC set a **daily central parity** — the "fix" — each morning, and the yuan could trade only within a 2% band above or below that level during the session. In practice, the PBoC used state-owned banks to ensure the yuan rarely reached the edges of the band. The fix itself was set by a panel of market-makers, but in a way that gave the PBoC near-total control over the direction of the currency.

This system had kept the yuan remarkably stable — and arguably overvalued — through years of strong Chinese growth. By 2015, however, several pressures were building simultaneously: GDP growth was slowing from its double-digit peaks toward 7%, exports were weakening, and a domestic stock-market bubble had burst spectacularly over the preceding two months. The gap between the PBoC's daily fix and where the yuan would have traded on a free market — the so-called "basis" — had widened meaningfully.

<div class="pt-callout info">
<span class="pt-callout-h">The "fix" and the market rate</span>
By mid-2015 the PBoC's daily fix was setting the yuan several tenths of a percent stronger than where offshore (CNH) markets were actually trading it. Closing this gap — whether for SDR credibility or market efficiency reasons — required a one-time step-down in the fix. The PBoC acknowledged as much in its August 11 announcement.
</div>

## What exactly happened on 11 August 2015?

On the morning of 11 August, the PBoC announced two things simultaneously: first, it was cutting the day's central parity by **1.9%** — the biggest single fix move since China ended its dual-currency system in January 1994; second, it was reforming the fixing mechanism so that future parities would take more account of the previous day's closing market rate.

The yuan weakened sharply as markets processed both pieces of news. The fix change was large enough to be news by itself; the methodology change signalled that more weakness might follow if markets pushed the yuan lower at close. Over the next two sessions — 12 and 13 August — the PBoC cut the fix by further increments, bringing the cumulative three-day depreciation to roughly **3%** against the dollar.

## What were China's motivations?

The PBoC's official explanation was straightforward: the fix had drifted away from the market rate, and the reform was needed to make the yuan more "market-determined" — a criterion the IMF had said was necessary for SDR inclusion. That case was credible: the <a href="https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2016/09/30/AM16-PR16440-IMF-Launches-New-SDR-Basket-Including-Chinese-Renminbi" target="_blank" rel="noopener">IMF did approve yuan inclusion</a> on 30 November 2015, with the yuan formally entering the SDR basket in October 2016 at a weight of about 10.92% — the first new currency added since the euro replaced the franc and deutschmark in 1999.

But analysts pointed to other pressures. China's export growth had turned negative in July 2015 for the first time in several years, and a weaker yuan would provide some relief. GDP growth was tracking toward its slowest pace in 25 years. The domestic equity bubble — the Shanghai Composite had fallen roughly 30% from its June 2015 peak — was damaging household wealth and confidence.

| Stated motivation | Market interpretation |
|---|---|
| Fix reform for SDR compliance | Plausible but convenient cover for a weaker-yuan policy |
| "Market-determined" exchange rate | Gave PBoC flexibility to move the yuan lower without announcing it |
| Aligning fix with market close | Amplified weakness: bad closes fed weaker next-day fixes |
| One-time technical adjustment | Markets feared it was the start of a multi-year devaluation trend |

The <a href="https://www.weforum.org/stories/2015/08/why-has-china-devalued-its-currency/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">World Economic Forum's contemporaneous analysis</a> noted that all three motivations were probably present simultaneously — the SDR case gave Beijing political cover for what was also an economically convenient move.

## What was the global market reaction?

The immediate global reaction was a sharp **risk-off** move. The reasoning was simple: if China was weakening its currency, it suggested either that Beijing was more worried about growth than its official data showed, or that it was launching a competitive devaluation — or both. Either way, the signal was bearish for global growth.

The **Australian dollar** was among the hardest-hit G10 currencies. As a [proxy for Chinese economic demand](/research/aud-china-proxy) — particularly for iron ore and bulk commodities — the AUD fell roughly 2% in the two days following the shock. It was already under pressure from falling iron ore prices; the yuan move accelerated the decline. The [NZD](/currency/nzd) followed closely given its own commodity-export exposure.

Equity markets sold off globally: the Dow fell 1.2% on 11 August, and the S&P 500 followed. The worst equity impact came later — in the "Black Monday 2.0" sell-off on 24 August 2015, when the S&P 500 fell roughly 11% from its pre-devaluation level before recovering.

In emerging markets, the reaction was more severe. Currencies from Indonesia to Brazil fell as investors asked whether China's weakness would spread through commodity prices and global trade. This [risk-off rotation](/research/risk-on-risk-off-currencies) was a textbook "China shock" — the world's second-largest economy sending a deflationary signal outward.

## What happened to China's reserves?

The devaluation did not calm capital outflows — it intensified them. Chinese households and corporations, seeing the yuan weaken and fearing further falls, converted renminbi savings into dollars at an accelerating pace. According to contemporaneous <a href="https://www.brookings.edu/articles/chinas-achilles-heel-capital-flight/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Brookings Institution analysis</a>, China suffered nearly $700 billion in capital flight in 2015 alone.

To prevent the yuan from falling further, the PBoC intervened heavily — selling dollars, buying yuan. Reserves that had peaked at nearly $4 trillion in June 2014 fell toward $3 trillion by early 2017. The PBoC also reimposed capital controls, tightened cross-border yuan flows, and jawboned the market with strong-fix signals. Gradually, the outflows abated, and the yuan stabilised.

## How does this episode fit into the broader currency-war narrative?

China's 2015 move was immediately framed in the media as a [currency war](/research/currency-wars-explained) escalation — Beijing joining the race to devalue started by Japan's aggressive quantitative easing under Abenomics and the ECB's own QE programme. That framing had some merit: a weaker yuan does boost Chinese export competitiveness. But the "currency war" narrative overstated the deliberateness of the move.

The PBoC was primarily trying to solve a domestic problem — a currency that had become increasingly overvalued as the dollar strengthened — while simultaneously satisfying the IMF's market-reform conditions for SDR inclusion. The collateral damage to global risk sentiment was, in that sense, an unintended consequence of a policy action that was both technically sound and badly communicated.

What the episode revealed about [currency intervention](/research/currency-intervention-explained) is instructive: even a large, credible central bank cannot simultaneously devalue and maintain confidence. The PBoC wanted a small, controlled depreciation. Markets, once they sensed the direction, pushed for much more — forcing the PBoC to spend over a trillion dollars defending the floor it had just cut.

For traders watching the [AUD/USD](/pair/audusd), the August 2015 event was a reminder that China-linked currencies can move sharply and quickly on PBoC signals — and that the fix announcement each morning is one of the most market-sensitive data points in Asian trading hours.

<div class="pt-callout warn">
<span class="pt-callout-h">Lesson for macro traders</span>
The yuan devaluation showed how a technically motivated policy reform can be misread by markets as a panic signal. When a central bank with heavy capital-flow controls suddenly reforms its exchange-rate mechanism, markets tend to assume the worst-case scenario — until the central bank spends enough reserves to prove otherwise.
