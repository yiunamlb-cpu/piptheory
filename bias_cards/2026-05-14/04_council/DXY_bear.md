The April CPI spike is a one-month noise that will be faded as the ECB’s accelerated hiking cycle compresses the US–EZ rate differential faster than the Fed can counteract with hawkish talk, driving DXY back to 99.00–99.50.

PRIMARY DRIVER:
  Theme: ECB Hawkish Pivot (Theme 2) > Fed Stuck‑at‑Neutral (Theme 3) — the widening relative‑rate path that originally fuelled the euro rally has not been displaced by a single CPI print; it is *accelerating*.
  Specialist support: The current Fed‑Watcher read (65% probability of a June cut) has been falsified by the hot CPI, but the hawkish repricing into a December hike is already over‑extended — the Fed’s reaction function still favours holding rates steady, not hiking again, given the lagged impact of the energy shock on demand. The Inflation Tracker’s stale data signal is now updated, but the market has priced a peak‑hawkish scenario that leaves no room for disappointment.
  Mechanism: The ECB has three fully priced rate hikes in front of it; the Fed has zero. As those June, July, and September hikes are delivered, the US–EZ 2‑year spread (currently ~120bp) will tighten towards 80bp — a powerful tailwind for EURUSD and headwind for DXY. Meanwhile, the hot CPI forces the Fed into “higher for longer”, but without an actual hike that repricing is shallow and likely fades by the June ECB meeting. The dollar’s safe‑haven bid from Iran merely delays the structural erosion; it does not reverse it.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE:
  1. ECB rate path: Kazimir’s “all but inevitable” June hike, plus market pricing for three hikes, implies deposit rate reaching 2.75% by Q4 — a level that materially chips away at the Fed’s rate advantage, even if the Fed stays at 3.5‑3.75%.

EURUSD positioning and structure: The 1.05→1.12 rally pricing three ECB hikes is not a sign of “fully priced” exhaustion — it is trend‑following, and the breakout above 1.10 is a structural level that has attracted real‑money flows. The Bull Advocate’s crowded‑long narrative is not supported by any concrete positioning data; even if speculative longs are extended, the macro‑fundamental bid remains intact.
  4. That scenario is dollar‑bearish, not bullish, as real yield differentials invert further.

BULL ENGAGEMENT:
  Strongest bull point against this bear case: The April CPI printed a clear upside surprise (3.5% headline, 2.9% core), which directly falsifies the “explicit easing bias” and, combined with FOMC dissenters, likely forces a hawkish minutes that reprices the Fed back to a December hike, triggering a durable dollar rally.
  Rebuttal: The CPI surprise is a snapshot, not a regime change. The Fed has been explicit that it looks through energy‑driven one‑off moves; core inflation at 2.9% is sticky but not accelerating at a pace that demands a hike. The Committee’s median dot has consistently over‑predicted hikes in recent years, and the market’s abrupt repricing from a 15% December‑hike probability to, say, 40% is already in the price at DXY 101. The bar for the minutes to *further* surprise hawkishly is now high. Meanwhile, the ECB is *actually* about to deliver a hike, not just talk about it, compressing the spread no matter what the Fed says. The bear case lives because the short‑dollar trade only needs the ECB to hike while the Fed holds; it does not need the Fed to cut. The Bull Advocate’s scenario requires the Fed to actively switch to a tightening bias, a higher bar that the data so far does not meet.

CONFIDENCE IN THIS BEAR CASE: 4/10
  Note: The hot CPI has materially damaged the bear conviction. This case can only work if the market’s hawkish Fed repricing reverses — a plausible but not base‑case outcome. The Judge must weight the probability accordingly.
