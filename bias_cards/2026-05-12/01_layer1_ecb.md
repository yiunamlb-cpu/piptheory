### KEY OBSERVATIONS  
- **A hike was actively debated but deferred.** Lagarde revealed that the Governing Council “debated, at length and in depth, a decision to possibly hike” alongside the unanimous decision to hold. The hold was justified by “insufficient information” – they want six weeks of post‑war data on second‑round effects, particularly wage negotiations, selling prices, and inflation expectations. This puts the ball squarely in the June meeting.  
- **Language drift is deliberate and hawkish.** The previous phrase describing risks as “tilted” has been deleted; risks are now framed as “upside risks to inflation … intensified” and “downside risks to growth … intensified”. The move from “tilted” to “intensified” signals that the previous model of a balanced‑but‑skewed assessment has been replaced by a sharper, two‑sided deterioration. Lagarde confirmed the direction: “I know where we’re heading”.  
- **No second‑round effects yet – but the clock is ticking.** Core inflation fell slightly (2.2 % in April) and services inflation decelerated to 3.0 %. The ECB’s wage tracker and surveys still point to easing labour costs. However, the fear is that energy prices – up 10.9 % y/y – will eventually feed into wages and broader prices the longer the conflict persists. The Council explicitly links the reaction function to the duration of the supply shock, not just its one‑off impact.  
- **Symmetry is reaffirmed, but not as a constraint on action.** Lagarde restated the three‑anchor reaction function: symmetric 2 % target, reaction depends on deviation type, and forceful action if large/sustained. This framework suggests the ECB is willing to look through a temporary energy spike, but the explicit talk of “persistent” risks and the removal of “tilted” imply they view the current shock as potentially large enough to warrant a “persistent” response – i.e., a rate hike.  
- **Periphery‑spread risk is not mentioned directly**, but the Vice‑President’s confident assessment of euro‑area bank liquidity and the absence of TPI references suggest that financial fragmentation is not a binding constraint right now. I could not confirm BTP‑Bund spreads; the available data is March Bund yield at 2.905 % and no Italy‑10y quote, so this is an inference.

### INVALIDATION  
The base case of a June hike would be upended if:  
1. Energy prices fall sharply and sustainably (conflict de‑escalation or rapid reopening of shipping routes), or  
2. Hard data shows a severe growth cliff (e.g. PMIs below 45, unemployment rising) that tips the Council toward risk‑management on growth, overriding the inflation‑upside concern, or  
3. Second‑round effects remain absent well into June despite elevated energy – i.e., wages and inflation expectations stay anchored, making the ECB comfortable that they can wait longer.
