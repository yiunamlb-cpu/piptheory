Probability: medium (a benign print is plausible given that the March jump was gasoline‑driven and April oil has eased slightly).  
Timing window: May 12 release; any dovish Fed speaker comment before then would add incremental support.  

TIMEFRAME: tactical (days to weeks)  
TARGET ZONE: 6200–6250 (recovery toward prior highs before the inflation scare)  
ENTRY ZONE: 5950–6000 (pullback into the strategist’s buy zone, just above the 5850 invalidation floor)  
INVALIDATION: a daily close below 5850. That level would signal the stagflation narrative is overwhelming the AI anchor, likely accompanied by a real growth scare or a hyperscaler capex cut.  

CONTRARIAN ENGAGEMENT:  
Strongest contrarian point: “Stagflation is historically the worst regime for equities, and the AI anchor narrative could break if sustained energy inflation forces a capex pause — a 5‑7% correction below 5850 is plausible.”  
Rebuttal: The stagflation playbook requires genuine economic stagnation, which this cycle lacks. U.S. growth is slowing but not stalling; employment remains healthy. Elevated inflation from supply‑side oil is very different from the 1970s wage‑price spiral, because real wages are not accelerating and productivity gains from AI are disinflationary in other sectors. The AI capex thesis is not a fragile narrative — it is backed by committed multi‑year capital budgets that are not economically sensitive to short‑term energy cost spikes, because data centre power contracts are hedged or locked in. The risk of a single hyperscaler cutting capex due to margin pressure exists, but in the current environment, any such cut would be more likely driven by overcapacity fears than by energy costs — and overcapacity is not yet a consensus concern. Thus, while a hot CPI could trigger a kick lower, the pullback would find aggressive buyers near 5850, keeping the tactical long thesis valid.  

CONFIDENCE IN THIS BULL CASE: 6/10  
Note: this reflects the internal coherence and evidence of the bull argument, not the probability that ES rallies. The case is moderate: it rests on the reflation‑vs‑stagflation distinction and the durability of AI anchoring; it acknowledges that the May 12 CPI is the make‑or‑break event.
