KEY OBSERVATIONS:
- **Risk‑assessment shift is the defining drift.** The statement dropped the word “tilted” and now states that upside risks to inflation and downside risks to growth have “intensified.” This is a clear hawkish turn relative to the previous balanced‑risk language, signalling the Council now views the inflation threat as material and persistent.
- **Hold was unanimous, but a hike was seriously debated.** Lagarde revealed that the Governing Council discussed hiking at length, yet the decision to leave rates unchanged was unanimous. This suggests a bridge between the hawks (Holzmann, Knot, Kazimir) and doves (Centeno, Villeroy) was found, but the bias is firmly toward tightening if second‑round effects appear.
- **Underlying inflation is cooling – a counterweight to the hawkish risk assessment.** Headline CPI jumped to 3.0 % on energy, but core HICP fell to 2.2 % and services inflation eased to 3.0 % (from 3.2 %). The ECB’s wage tracker still points to easing labour costs, and the corporate telephone survey shows no intention of large wage increases. This “inconsistent information” gives the Council room to wait.
- **Financial tightening is doing part of the work.** Lagarde noted that some market‑driven tightening has occurred, implying the effective stance is already a touch tighter than the official rate suggests. This reduces the urgency to pre‑commit to a hike.
- **Periphery‑spread risk not mentioned, but energy‑driven fiscal strains could resurface.** No language on the TPI or fragmentation, but if the war persists and BTP–Bund spreads widen, the ECB’s ability to hike aggressively would be constrained. We need a fresh BTP–Bund 10y observation to gauge this.

GAP vs MARKET:
  The ECB’s language is slightly more hawkish than market pricing (which already leaned toward a June hike but was tentative). The Council’s openness to hiking — and the explicit debate — reinforces the hike‑bias. However, Lagarde’s insistence that second‑round effects are absent cautions against an imminent move. We see a mild misalignment: the ECB is leaning more hawkishly than the current market probability of ~60 % implies, though the gap may have narrowed since April 30.
  Trade implication: Fading EUR weakness could be a modest long‑EUR play if the market re‑prices the June‑hike probability higher. Peripheral spreads (BTP–Bund) would come under pressure on a sustained tightening cycle, so positions that are long Bunds vs BTPs may benefit.
