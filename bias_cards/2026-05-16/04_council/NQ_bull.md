  Rebuttal: The stagflation risk is real, but it requires *both* a sustained oil shock *and* a Fed policy error. Currently the Fed has held rates firm and is leaning toward a “wait and see” approach; the majority of FOMC members are not on the hawkish‑dissenter side. Additionally, tech multiples have been resilient even with 10‑year yields already elevated vs. 2021. While a “hot CPI + hawkish Fed speak” combination would challenge the bull case, it is not the base case – the bull case survives as long as the CPI prints in line with expectations and the Fed language remains steady. The probability of a 2022‑style meltdown is lower than the contrarian estimates; the AI demand story is demonstrably stronger than anything that existed in 2022, and corporate balance sheets are healthier. Therefore, the pullback‑buy strategy is still valid, with invalidation strictly defined.

CONFIDENCE IN THIS BULL CASE: 5/10
  Note: This is the strength of the *case*, not the probability of winning. The narrative supporting longs is plausible and fits the current macro, but it is fragile and heavily dependent on no stagflation shock materialising in the next two weeks. That keeps the case at moderate strength; it is a tactical, not a high‑conviction, structural long.

NARRATIVE SUMMARY (3-5 sentences):
  NQ remains the premier equity vehicle for AI capex exposure, and the market has yet to price any slowdown in that spending cycle. The Fed’s neutral stance prevents a valuation re‑rating lower; pullbacks have been consistently bought, and there is no technical evidence that this pattern is breaking. While the energy‑driven inflation spike is a threat, it has not yet triggered a hawkish pivot severe enough to kill the growth trade. A well‑timed entry on the next dip, with a tight invalidation below the swing low, offers an attractive risk‑reward. For now, the burden of proof is on the bears to show that the AI cycle is ending or the Fed is about to hike — neither condition is met.
