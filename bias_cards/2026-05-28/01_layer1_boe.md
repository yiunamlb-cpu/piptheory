KEY OBSERVATIONS:  

- The March meeting was entirely shaped by the outbreak of conflict in the Middle East and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which sent oil and gas prices ~60% higher. The BoE judged this as a significant negative supply shock with direct, indirect, and second‑round inflationary effects.  
- Even though the disinflation process had continued before the shock (services inflation still elevated at 4.4%, wages moderating), multiple members – Breeden, Ramsden, Taylor, and Dhingra – explicitly stated they would have voted for a 25bp cut this meeting absent the conflict. That reveals the underlying trend was turning dovish before being abruptly halted.  
- The minutes and individual vote rationales display a high‑contingency, data‑dependent posture: the Committee deliberately paused to gather more information on the scale and duration of the shock. No one argued for an immediate hike, but several (Mann, Pill, Greene) flagged serious upside inflation risks and the potential for second‑round effects to force a more restrictive stance down the line.  
- A notable phrase: “policy would need to be **less restrictive** if the shock was very short‑lived … conversely would require a **more restrictive** stance if second‑round effects intensify.” This symmetric conditionality, combined with the unanimous vote, underscores a committee that is holding not because it is lost, but because the range of plausible outcomes is extraordinarily wide.  
- The CPI near‑term projection was revised up sharply – to 3‑3.5% over the next couple of quarters vs. a previous expectation of a decline to ~2% from April. The energy price path, the Ofgem cap reset in July, and the risk that households’ inflation sensitivity is elevated after years of above‑target prints were all highlighted as factors that could anchor inflation expectations higher.
